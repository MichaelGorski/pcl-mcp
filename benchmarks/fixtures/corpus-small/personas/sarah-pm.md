# Sarah Chen — Agency Project Manager

Sarah Chen is the primary persona for TaskPilot. She represents the mid-career project manager who has outgrown spreadsheets and basic Trello boards but finds enterprise tools like Jira or Asana overwhelming and overpriced for her team size. She manages a team of four to six people at a digital agency that handles web development, branding, and marketing campaigns for clients ranging from local businesses to mid-market SaaS companies.

## Daily Reality

Sarah's typical day starts at 8:30 AM with a coffee and a mental inventory of what is due this week. She currently opens three tabs — Trello for task status, Google Sheets for time tracking, and Slack for team updates — just to assemble a picture of where things stand. By the time she has context on all active projects, it is often 9:15 AM and she has not yet done any productive work. Her biggest frustration is that her tools do not talk to each other, so she is the human integration layer.

## Decision-Making Behavior

Sarah evaluates new tools primarily on speed-to-value. She will sign up for a free trial, and if she cannot accomplish something meaningful within the first session, she will not return. She is not opposed to learning new software — she actually enjoys it — but she has been burned too many times by tools that promised simplicity and delivered complexity. She makes purchasing decisions for her team and reports the cost to the agency owner, so price sensitivity is real but not extreme. Value justification matters more than absolute cost.

## Pain Points in Detail

Her most acute pain point is client communication. Three of her current clients want weekly status updates, but assembling those updates takes 30-45 minutes per client per week. She copies data from Trello, formats it in Google Docs, and emails it as a PDF. A tool that could auto-generate a client-visible progress view would save her roughly six hours per month — and that is the kind of concrete ROI she can pitch to her boss.

Her second biggest pain point is capacity planning. When a new project lands, she needs to know who has bandwidth. Currently she asks each team member individually via Slack, which is slow and unreliable. She wants a dashboard that shows each person's allocated hours versus available hours for the current and next sprint.

## What Success Looks Like for Sarah

If TaskPilot works for Sarah, her morning routine drops from 45 minutes to 5 minutes: she opens a single dashboard, sees a red/yellow/green status for each project, and knows immediately where to focus. Client updates happen automatically or with a single click. She can onboard a new contractor to a project by sharing a link rather than writing a two-page briefing document. Her team tracks time passively (timers linked to tasks) rather than filling out timesheets on Friday afternoon from memory. She feels in control rather than constantly catching up.

## Technical Expectations

Sarah expects mobile responsiveness as a baseline, not a feature. She checks project status from her phone during commutes and client meetings. She expects integrations with Google Calendar (for deadlines), Slack (for notifications), and ideally Xero or QuickBooks (for invoicing). She does not care about the underlying technology stack but is impressed by fast page loads and a clean, uncluttered interface.
